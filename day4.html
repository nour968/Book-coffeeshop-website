Agatha Christie

Agatha Christie (1890–1976) was an English writer widely regarded as the “Queen of Crime.” She created some of the world’s most famous fictional detectives, including Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple, and her works remain among the best-selling books of all time, second only to the Bible and Shakespeare. Her mastery of mystery, suspense, and clever plot twists redefined crime fiction. Christie’s novels, such as Murder on the Orient Express, And Then There Were None, and The Murder of Roger Ackroyd, showcase her ability to keep readers guessing until the very end. She also wrote successful plays, including The Mousetrap, which holds the record as the world’s longest-running play. Through her intricate storytelling and unforgettable characters, Agatha Christie left an enduring legacy as one of literature’s greatest storytellers.

Known for her clever plots, surprising twists, and timeless storytelling, Christie’s novels continue to captivate readers across generations.
